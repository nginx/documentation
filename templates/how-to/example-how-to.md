## Overview

Use this guide to brew a single cup of pour-over coffee from whole beans. Pour-over brewing forces hot water through a bed of ground coffee in a controlled, manual pour. The result is a clear, aromatic cup with no sediment. This method works with any cone-shaped pour-over brewer, including the V60 and the Chemex.

---

## Before you begin

Before you begin, ensure you have:

- **A pour-over brewer and a matching paper filter**: The filter must fit your specific brewer model. Using the wrong size causes the filter to collapse during brewing.
- **Whole coffee beans**: Use beans roasted within the past four weeks. Older beans produce a flat, stale cup.
- **A burr grinder**: A burr grinder produces consistent particle size. A blade grinder produces uneven grounds, which causes over-extraction and bitterness.
- **A gooseneck kettle**: A gooseneck spout gives you precise control over the pour. A standard kettle is difficult to control and often floods the grounds.
- **A kitchen scale accurate to 1 gram**: Measuring by weight produces consistent results. Measuring by volume (scoops or tablespoons) does not.
- **A timer**: Extraction time directly affects flavour. Most pour-over recipes target a total brew time of three to three and a half minutes.

---

## Grind and weigh the coffee

1. Weigh 15 grams of whole coffee beans on the kitchen scale.

2. Set the burr grinder to a medium-coarse setting. The grounds should resemble coarse sea salt. A setting that is too fine produces over-extracted, bitter coffee; a setting that is too coarse produces weak, sour coffee.

3. Grind the beans immediately before brewing. Ground coffee begins losing its aromatics within minutes of grinding.

---

## Prepare the brewer

1. Place the paper filter inside the pour-over brewer.

2. Rinse the filter with hot water directly from the kettle. Rinsing removes the papery taste from the filter and pre-heats the brewer, which helps maintain a stable brewing temperature.

3. Discard the rinse water from the vessel or mug below the brewer.

4. Add the ground coffee to the rinsed filter. Gently shake the brewer to level the coffee bed. An even coffee bed ensures uniform water flow during brewing.

---

## Brew the coffee

Heat the water to between 90°C and 96°C (194°F to 205°F). Water that is too hot scorches the grounds; water that is too cool under-extracts them.

1. Start the timer.

2. Pour 30 grams of water evenly over the grounds in a slow, circular motion. This first pour is called the bloom. The grounds will bubble and expand as trapped carbon dioxide escapes. The bloom indicates the beans are fresh.

3. Wait 30 seconds for the bloom to complete.

4. Continue pouring water in slow, steady circles, keeping the water level roughly 1 cm below the top of the filter. Pour in stages of 50 to 70 grams at a time, pausing between each pour to let the water draw down. Target a total water weight of 250 grams.

5. Stop pouring when the scale reads 250 grams total. The entire brew should finish draining in three to three and a half minutes. If the brew drains faster, grind finer next time. If it drains slower, grind coarser.

6. Remove the brewer and filter from the mug. Discard the spent grounds and the filter.

## Troubleshooting

### The coffee tastes bitter

**Symptom**: The finished cup is harsh, dry, or astringent.

**Cause**: The coffee is over-extracted. Water spent too long in contact with the grounds, or the grind is too fine.

**Fix**: Coarsen the grind by one or two steps on the burr grinder. If the brew time was longer than three and a half minutes, also try pouring slightly faster.

### The coffee tastes sour or weak

**Symptom**: The finished cup is thin, sharp, or acidic in an unpleasant way.

**Cause**: The coffee is under-extracted. Water passed through the grounds too quickly, or the grind is too coarse.

**Fix**: Fine the grind by one or two steps on the burr grinder. If the brew time was shorter than two and a half minutes, also try pouring slightly more slowly.

### The filter collapses during brewing

**Symptom**: The paper filter folds inward and blocks the flow of water.

**Cause**: The filter was not seated correctly, or the initial rinse did not press the filter against the brewer walls.

**Fix**: Remove the brewer from the mug, re-seat the filter, and press it firmly against the brewer walls before adding the grounds. Rinse again to re-seal the filter in place.
