## Overview

In this tutorial, you will calibrate a burr grinder to a medium-coarse setting, prepare a pour-over brewer, and brew a 250 ml cup of coffee from whole beans. You will also evaluate the result and adjust your technique for the next brew.

It is intended for home brewers who are new to manual brewing methods. It assumes that you have basic knowledge of:

- [How drip coffee works](../concepts/drip-coffee-extraction.md) — how water temperature and contact time affect flavor.
- [Types of coffee roast](../concepts/coffee-roasts.md) — the difference between light, medium, and dark roasts and how they affect grind size.

By the end of this tutorial, you will be able to:

- Calibrate a burr grinder to a target grind size for pour-over brewing.
- Prepare a pour-over brewer and filter correctly before brewing.
- Execute a bloom pour and a staged fill pour to achieve an even extraction.
- Evaluate the finished cup and identify one grind or pour adjustment for the next brew.

---

## Background

Pour-over brewing is a manual method in which hot water is poured slowly and deliberately over a bed of ground coffee held in a cone-shaped filter. The brewer controls every variable — water temperature, pour rate, and total brew time — which makes it possible to produce a very clean, flavorful cup. It also makes it possible to make consistent mistakes, which is why calibration and technique matter.

A burr grinder crushes beans between two abrasive surfaces to produce grounds of a consistent size. Consistent particle size is essential for even extraction: if some particles are fine and some are coarse, the fine ones over-extract and turn bitter before the coarse ones have finished extracting.

---

## Before you begin

Before you begin, ensure you have:

- **A pour-over brewer and a matching paper filter**: The filter must fit your brewer model. Common brewer and filter pairs are the Hario V60 with a V60 filter and the Chemex with a Chemex filter. Using the wrong filter size causes it to collapse during brewing.
- **A burr grinder**: A burr grinder produces consistent particle size. A blade grinder produces uneven grounds that cause over-extraction and bitterness. See [Choose a burr grinder](../guides/choose-burr-grinder.md) for buying guidance.
- **Whole coffee beans, roasted within the past four weeks**: Older beans produce a flat, stale cup. Check the roast date on the bag.
- **A gooseneck kettle**: A gooseneck spout gives precise control over the pour direction and rate. A standard kettle is difficult to control and tends to flood the grounds.
- **A kitchen scale accurate to 1 gram**: Measuring by weight produces consistent results across brews. Measuring by volume (scoops or tablespoons) does not.
- **A timer**: Extraction time directly affects flavor. Target a total brew time of three to three and a half minutes.

---

## Calibrate the grinder

Calibrating the grinder before each session ensures the grind size matches the brewing method. Pour-over brewing requires a medium-coarse grind — coarser than espresso, finer than cold brew.

1. Place an empty container under the grinder chute.

2. Set the grinder to the medium-coarse position on the adjustment dial. On most home burr grinders this is between 15 and 20 on a 40-step dial. Consult your grinder's manual if you are unsure of the scale.

3. Run five grams of whole beans through the grinder to purge any grounds left from the previous session.

    Purging removes stale grounds that would otherwise mix with the fresh batch and affect the flavor of the first cup.

4. Examine the purged grounds. Rub a small amount between your fingers.

    The grounds should feel like coarse sea salt — gritty but not powdery. If the grounds feel like fine sand, move the dial one step coarser. If they feel like gravel, move the dial one step finer.

5. Discard the purge grounds. The grinder is now calibrated for this session.

---

## Prepare the brewer

Preparing the brewer correctly before adding coffee removes the papery taste of the filter and pre-heats the vessel, which helps maintain a stable brewing temperature.

1. Weigh 15 grams of whole coffee beans on the kitchen scale.

2. Grind the beans immediately before brewing. Ground coffee begins losing its aromatics within minutes of grinding.

    ```text
    Grinder output: approximately 2 tablespoons of medium-coarse grounds
    ```

3. Place the paper filter inside the pour-over brewer. Press the filter firmly against the brewer walls so it sits flat with no folded edges. A filter that sits away from the walls collapses inward during brewing and blocks water flow.

4. Set the brewer on top of your mug or carafe.

5. Pour hot water slowly around the inside of the filter to rinse it. Use enough water to soak the filter completely — approximately 50 ml.

    Rinsing removes the papery taste from the filter and pre-heats the brewer and mug. A cold brewer drops the water temperature below the target range as soon as brewing starts.

6. Discard the rinse water from the mug or carafe.

7. Add the ground coffee to the rinsed filter. Gently shake the brewer side to side to level the coffee bed.

    An even coffee bed ensures water flows through the grounds uniformly during brewing. An uneven bed causes channelling, where water finds the path of least resistance and under-extracts most of the grounds.

---

## Brew the coffee

Heat the kettle water to between 90°C and 96°C (194°F to 205°F). Water below this range under-extracts the grounds and produces a sour cup. Water above this range scorches the grounds and produces a bitter cup.

1. Place the brewer and mug on the scale. Zero the scale.

2. Start the timer.

3. Pour 30 grams of water slowly and evenly over the grounds in a small circular motion, starting from the centre and moving outward. This first pour is called the bloom.

    The grounds will bubble and expand as trapped carbon dioxide — produced during roasting and released during brewing — escapes. The bloom indicates the beans are fresh. If there is no bubbling, the beans are likely stale.

4. Wait 30 seconds for the bloom to complete. Do not pour during this time.

5. Continue pouring water in slow, steady circles. Pour in stages of 50 to 70 grams, pausing between each pour to let the water draw down to just above the level of the grounds. Keep the total water weight target at 250 grams.

    Pouring in stages gives the grounds time to release their soluble compounds evenly. Flooding the brewer in a single pour reduces contact time and produces a weaker, uneven cup.

6. Stop pouring when the scale reads 250 grams total. Note the time when the last of the water has drained through the filter.

    Expected result: the total brew time from the start of the bloom to the last drip is between three and three and a half minutes. If the brew drains faster than three minutes, the grind is too coarse. If it drains slower than three and a half minutes, the grind is too fine.

7. Remove the brewer and filter from the mug. Discard the spent grounds and the filter.

---

## Evaluate the result

Evaluating the cup immediately after brewing helps you identify one specific adjustment to make for the next brew.

1. Take a small sip while the coffee is still hot enough to taste clearly — around 60°C to 70°C.

2. Identify the dominant characteristic using the guide below.

    | Characteristic | Likely cause | Adjustment |
    |---|---|---|
    | Bitter, dry, or astringent | Over-extracted: grind too fine or brew time too long | Move grinder one step coarser |
    | Sour, thin, or sharp | Under-extracted: grind too coarse or brew time too short | Move grinder one step finer |
    | Flat or lacking aroma | Beans are stale or water temperature too low | Use fresher beans or raise kettle temperature |
    | Balanced, sweet, and clean | Correct extraction | No adjustment needed |

3. Write down the grinder setting, water temperature, and your evaluation note. Keeping a brew log makes it possible to repeat successful brews and avoid repeating unsuccessful ones.
